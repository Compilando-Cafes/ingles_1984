About the Book

George Orwell’s 1984 is a dystopian novel that paints a chilling portrait of a totalitarian regime where freedom of thought is forbidden, history is rewritten, and surveillance is absolute. The story follows Winston Smith, a low-ranking member of the Party, who secretly dreams of rebellion in a world where independent thinking is the most dangerous crime of all. As Winston begins to question the system, he is pulled into a spiral of fear, deception, and psychological control that forces him to confront the terrifying power of the state.

Far from being just a work of fiction, 1984 is a prophetic warning — a story that resonates even more powerfully today as the boundaries between truth and manipulation blur. It's not just a novel: it's a survival manual for the mind.

About the Author

George Orwell (1903–1950), born Eric Arthur Blair, was a British novelist, journalist, and political thinker best known for his fierce criticism of authoritarianism and his defense of truth and freedom. Having witnessed the rise of totalitarian regimes in Europe and participated in the Spanish Civil War, Orwell used his experiences to craft powerful works that warned against censorship, propaganda, and the erosion of individual liberty.

His writing style was clear, direct, and deeply human. Besides 1984, Orwell also wrote Animal Farm, a satirical allegory that critiques the corruption of revolutionary ideals. Today, Orwell’s name is synonymous with political awareness and vigilance: "Orwellian" has become a term used worldwide to describe oppressive government practices and manipulation of truth.
